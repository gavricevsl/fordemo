INDIA — MARKET ANALYSIS

Semaglutide Loss of Exclusivity in India: A Market Reset in GLP-1 Therapies

In early March 2026, India lost exclusivity on semaglutide, the active ingredient of the most important blockbuster drug in the global GLP-1 market, represented by Novo Nordisk's drugs Ozempic and Wegovy. This event has unleashed an unprecedented wave of generic competition that has fundamentally changed the global pharmaceutical landscape, with prices for semaglutide falling by 80–90% (from ₹15,000 to ₹3,000) representing reductions of up to 90% in 60 days.

This is not just exciting news. It is the rapid transformation of a premium global therapy into a mass market.

Section 4

India's Generic Response at Scale

Indian manufacturers scaled generic semaglutide production within weeks of LOE.

The exclusivity loss had an immediate knock-on effect across India's pharma sector. Major manufacturers including Sun Pharma, Dr Reddy's, Lupin, and Aurobindo Strategic Health each took on the challenge to expand manufacturing operations to meet projected demand for hospitals and clinics.

Dr Reddy's became a brand: distribution of more than 60 million injectable pens within first quarter alone, sufficient supply for tens of thousands of patients.

In a remarkable demonstration of organizational power, Indian pharmaceutical firms have shifted from research-and-development partners to global cost leaders, supplying not just India but increasingly export markets across Asia and Africa.

Risks and Regulatory Pressure

Rapid generic rollout brings non-trivial risks.

Regulators, especially those swiftly increasing in volume in India in 2026, have reported that under-suspect manufacturers may not adequately attend to long-running and short-running stability of semaglutide injectables.

Section 8

Strategic Implications

01

For Novo Nordisk

India represents the front line of broader patent expiration challenges. Maintaining premium pricing globally requires demonstrating ongoing innovation, next-generation formulations, and stronger value evidence to justify the price gap.

India's playbook is now a reference point — generic substitution at scale can dramatically reduce the cost of chronic-disease therapy without compromising patient outcomes.
